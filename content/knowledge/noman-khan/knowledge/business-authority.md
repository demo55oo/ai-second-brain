## Section 1: Positioning & Niche Clarity

*Purpose: Defines your identity, direction, and unique positioning.*

### What core problem does your business solve, and why does it matter right now in your industry?

I solve the fragmentation between product, sales, and customer success that causes companies to leak revenue, lose customers, and stall growth.

### Who do you serve best, and what makes them the perfect fit for your approach?

I serve high-growth SaaS and cybersecurity companies that are under pressure to improve retention, accelerate adoption, and turn customer success into a measurable revenue engine.

Ideal target organizations that recognize the need for disciplined operating models, stronger cross-functional alignment, and a clearer path to profitable, scalable customer value.

### In one clear line, what do you want to be known for in your space?

The strategist who eliminates churn, strengthens retention, and scales customer-led growth.

### How is your method or approach different from others tackling the same challenge?

I have the ability to combine strategy with operational execution while designing customer success and services models that don't just look good on paper but actually scale, produce revenue, and withstand real-world complexity.

I have built frameworks that connect product adoption, value delivery, and commercial outcomes into a single, measurable system that improves retention and drives growth.

### What key belief or principle drives your work — something most people in your field overlook?

I have a core belief that customer success is not an operational function, it is rather a strategic engine that can shape how a company builds, sells, and delivers its product. In order to do this effectively you have to focus on outcomes. The real measure of customer success is whether customers achieve meaningful business results that tie directly to revenue, retention, and long-term growth.

## Section 3: ICP Refinement (Ideal Client Profile)

*Purpose: Extracts emotional triggers, stage-specific pain points, and audience clarity.*

### Describe your ideal client in one line — who they are, and what they want.

Not sure if there is only one type. But I see the following:

1. **Executive** — High-growth SaaS or cybersecurity company that wants to strengthen retention, scale customer success, and turn their customer experience into a predictable revenue engine.
2. **PE / Board-Focused** — PE-backed or rapidly scaling technology company seeking a disciplined operating model that boosts GRR, NRR, and overall customer lifetime value.
3. **Founder** — CEO who knows their customer engine isn't performing the way it should and needs clarity, structure, and a path to durable recurring revenue.

### What mistakes or wrong turns do they try before discovering your approach?

Most companies will go through a series of wrong turns: from hiring more CSMs, buying more tools, or pushing out new playbooks. Most of these will not fix the underlying issues in their customer lifecycle.

### What makes them hesitate to act, and what finally gets them to move?

I find that most leaders will hesitate to act because they fear disruption, doubt their internal readiness, and worry that changing their customer operating model will expose deeper issues they're not prepared to confront.

As for taking action, I see that if there is no inaction then it is a bigger problem for them, so most will start somewhere as cost of not doing anything is much higher.

## Section 5: Content · Niche, Subtopics & Voice

*Purpose: Defines your strategic message, tone, and reference style for scalable, non-repetitive content.*

### What's your main niche or industry focus?

Primary niche is high-growth SaaS and cybersecurity companies, especially those navigating scale, transformation, or post-acquisition complexity.

Focus on organizations that need stronger retention, revenue-aligned customer success, and a unified operating model across product, sales, services, and customer experience.

### What 3 topics could you talk about forever — the ones that define your expertise? (Evergreen Pillars)

1. **Turning Customer Success Into a Revenue Engine** — How to build a revenue-aligned operating model, strengthen GRR/NRR, accelerate adoption, and design customer lifecycles that directly influence renewal, expansion, and margin.
2. **Scaling Global Post-Sales Organizations Through Transformation** — The playbook for unifying services, support, CS, product, and sales across regions, especially during M&A, leadership shifts, and rapid growth, without disrupting operations or customers.
3. **Using Data, Product Usage, and Customer Outcomes to Drive Predictable Growth** — How telemetry, value realization, and outcome frameworks create clarity, eliminate guesswork, and enable companies to forecast, retain, and expand customers with precision.

### What 2 new areas or ideas are you currently exploring or testing? (Growth Pillars)

An area that I am intrigued by is **AI-Driven Customer Success Predictive Models**.

There are a handful of companies that are using machine-learning models that anticipate churn, expansion likelihood, adoption blockers, and customer health with far greater precision than traditional CS scoring. This includes integrating product telemetry, usage patterns, sentiment analysis, and services data to create a forward-looking, automated view of customer risk and opportunity.

### What 2 common myths or bad ideas in your field do you completely disagree with? (Controversial Pillars)

**Myth 1: "Customer Success is a support function, not a revenue driver."**

Most companies still operate as if customer success exists to answer tickets, run QBRs, and "keep customers happy." I really do not see it this way.

Customer Success is a commercial engine that can drive retention, expansion, and long-term customer value. This can be done by having CS aligned with product, sales, and services. It then can become one of the most predictable and scalable revenue channels in the business.

**Myth 2: "More tools or more CSMs will fix churn."**

There is an ideology in the market where leaders think churn comes from a lack of technology or not enough people. That is not true.

I have always felt that churn is almost always the result of poor operating models, unclear value realization, siloed teams, or weak handoffs — and certainly not the number of tools or headcount.

### What are 2–3 "light-bulb" lessons from your experience that always surprise people?

This thinking has evolved over time, but here are a few:

1. **Churn doesn't start at renewal — it starts at onboarding.** Most leaders think churn is a renewal problem. The real truth: churn is a time-to-value problem usually created in the first 30–90 days. This was further validated in a conversation with **Recurly** in a recent meeting in Boston. If onboarding is unclear, slow, or misaligned with expected outcomes, renewal risk is already being created long before anyone notices. Fixing onboarding often fixes churn without adding tools, CSMs, or pressure.
2. **Customer success is not a department — it's a system.** Companies assume CS sits between support and sales. I believe CS is actually the integration layer of the entire business. You have to look at CS as one of the nodes to the commercial engine. We know product, sales, services, market and support in this engine — why not CS.
3. **Data doesn't matter unless it changes behavior.** Most organizations use too many dashboards to review usage data, sentiment scores, and health metrics. You really have to measure what really matters and triggers actions. Measuring KPIs that drive ARR, NRR, and top revenue are key.

### What's one message you want your audience to remember you for? (North Star Message)

Customer Success isn't a support function — it's one of the most powerful revenue engines a company has, and it should be built, measured, and led like one.

### What recurring patterns or trends do you see emerging in your industry that your audience should prepare for?

There are a handful of patterns. I believe organisations that can align product, sales, services, and customer success around outcomes — and not just activities — will win.

1. Retention Is Becoming the Primary Growth Strategy
2. Customer Success Will Shift From "Support" to "Commercial Ownership"
3. Product Usage and Telemetry Will Replace Gut-Based Decision Making
4. AI Will Automate 40–60% of Traditional CS Motion
5. Services and CS Will Converge Into a Unified Value Organization
6. Partner Ecosystems Will Become Critical to Scale
7. Outcome-Based Pricing Will Replace Traditional Services Models
