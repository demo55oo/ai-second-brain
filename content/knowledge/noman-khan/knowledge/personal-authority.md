# Personal Authority Storytelling Task

## Goal of the Task

This task helps you build personal authority by sharing the real stories behind who you are. You'll reflect on life moments, lessons, and experiences that shaped the way you think, lead, and work today, both personally and professionally.

## Purpose

Each question is designed to draw out meaningful stories that show:

- What shaped your values and worldview
- How you handle challenges and growth
- What makes your approach or mindset unique

Your answers will serve as the foundation for story-driven content that fosters trust, connection, and authority with your audience.

## Step-by-Step Action Guide

1. **Choose a Question** — Pick one of the 30 questions listed below.
2. **Use ChatGPT to Answer** — Paste the question into ChatGPT.
3. **Use Voice In or Wispr Flow** — Speak your answer naturally. Aim to tell a story, as if talking to a close friend.
4. **Speak in Detail** — Don't rush. Share context, emotions, lessons learned, and how the experience impacted you. The more details you give, the better the story will become.
5. **Summarize with ChatGPT** — After finishing your answer, say: *"Hey ChatGPT, summarize this in detail and turn it into a story."* Review the summary to ensure it accurately captures your message.
6. **Finalize & Save** — Copy the final story and paste it under the relevant question in your Google Doc.
7. **Repeat** — Repeat this process for all 30 questions.

### Pro Tip

If one question sparks more than one story, capture them all. Every story adds another layer to your brand voice and authority.

## 30 Personal Authority Questions

### Phase 1: Brand Foundation

**Goal:** Show who you are, what shaped you, and what you stand for.

#### What's one moment from your past that changed the way you look at life or work?

I'll give you two examples. One would be when I moved to the Middle East with my parents — I must have been around 2 years old. I believe that was the early stage of my time where I would evolve into what I have become today. While it's a distant memory, I do realize it was the time where a lot of my thinking and ideologies were shaped, living in the Middle East and the United Kingdom.

From a schooling perspective, I went to a Catholic School and then a Department of Defense School in Bahrain. I had access to about 55 different nationalities, and most of my friends came from all across the world, including the Middle East. My values got molded because I was able to accept different perspectives and different people.

Moving to the United States was not a culture shock for me due to the fact that I had all this exposure, which aligned with the same values and aspirations.

#### Who taught you something you still live by today?

I think I would say that my granddad from my father's side is the person that I always looked up to, even though I did not spend too much time with him due to the fact we lived far away in a different country. But anytime I did meet with him — a humble and sweet man, very calm and collected, and very kind to everybody — I think that is something that has always stuck with me: to have empathy and understanding of how other people think.

#### When did you first realize what you do now really matters to you?

I'm not sure how to answer this question. I think it will continue to be a discovery for the remainder of my life. But the work that I've been doing over the last 15 to 20 years does really matter to me. I've been focused on go-to-market and customer success for companies that are moving from a perpetual licensing model to SaaS — companies that are using a SaaS software model.

#### What's a tough time you went through that made you stronger as a person or leader?

There are several things that happened in my life that make me a strong person. Like anyone, we all have big dreams. I arrived in the US in 1987, just around the time before the crash of 1987, and unfortunately it ended up having a domino effect on the family from a financial perspective and also from a life perspective. My dad lost his job. We were all in college; we all dropped out. And during that time frame we also lost a young brother to suicide, probably around 1991. We lost our home, became displaced, and lived in a hotel for a couple of months until we were able to find a home that we could rent. All the siblings dropped out of college and started to work. It was a difficult time mentally and physically just to deal with the losses that occurred.

During this time I also ended up buying a business for a couple of years, and it worked for a while and then it didn't, which ultimately forced me to file for bankruptcy and really just start over. Then, through the passage of time, we started to get better jobs and the financial situation became a little better. I met Fiona during this time frame, in the mid-90s, which shifted my focus towards figuring out my life.

While these challenges were hard, I think it shaped me into the person that I am today: to have a growth mindset, accept and understand the challenges, and just figure out as you go along how best to adapt.

#### What value from your family or childhood still guides how you work or treat people?

I'm going to go back again to my granddad, who taught me to be kind to people, understanding, and show empathy. I also think being honest and truthful provides continued relationships.

#### When did you take a risk that scared you, and what came out of it?

I think the risks that I have taken would be starting my own business, learning and running the business, and then ultimately losing the business — which was an eye-opener on what not to do. I think the other thing that had me scared was going back to school and finishing my bachelor's and then finishing my master's. It was a significant sacrifice for the family, but in the end what came out of it was I felt a little bit more secure in my credentials, and it also helped me with my career.

#### Tell me about a mistake that hurt you then but helped you later.

I think the mistake that I can point to in my professional career would be when I was putting an organization through a transformation of taking certain parts of the operational aspect of the business and moving it to different parts of the world, ultimately being closer to the customer. I was pretty effective in doing it for Asia, the Middle East, and certain parts of Europe. But there is one part of Europe that for some reason I did not pay close attention to — the overall needs of the team — which ultimately created an exodus of the organization, requiring me to rebuild the team from scratch. The lesson that I took away from it was: pay close attention to the business, inspect, trust, and then re-engage and repeat the cycle.

#### What was the moment you thought, "This is exactly what I'm meant to do"?

I have many moments like this, but if I think about it from a career perspective, I would say that working with a team that I can grow, coach, mentor, and ultimately have them succeed is something that I've always enjoyed.

#### What's a story from your early career that still shapes how you lead or make choices?

I think a story early from my career would be leading a team through change over multiple years. What I learned was that communication, guidance, and information updates are really important for leaders to succeed, and I've used that model across all of my career.

#### Who believed in you before you did, and what did that change for you?

I think Fiona would be the one that I would say has always believed in me and pushed me to do better personally, professionally, and financially — which basically gave me a disciplined approach of showing up and being very consistent, and the results will speak for themselves.

#### What's a small win that made you feel you were finally on the right path?

I think the small win that I can reflect back on that had me feeling I was on the right path is when I got promoted at Imperva to run the customer success organization. At that time I felt that I had the ability to manage and lead a fairly large organization.

#### When did you have to stand by your values, even when it wasn't easy?

During my time at Imperva, I was working with the leader of Asia who was pushing me to hire people in the region so he could sell the overall solution — which included software and services — claiming that he had a pipeline of $13 million. I professionally did not believe he could achieve those results, and I stood by my process of saying we need to see the pipeline materialize before we can start hiring. This created significant friction in the business, and the trust that we had between each other was severed.

#### How do you keep your calm or confidence when life feels uncertain?

This one is a tough one, but I would say prayer and fitness is what helps me stay confident and feel strong during uncertain times.

### Phase 2: Awareness & Authority

**Goal:** Turn lessons into insights that teach, guide, or challenge how people think.

#### What's one common mistake you see people make in your field?

I think a common mistake that is made in my field is leaders that are not curious and deliberate in their actions — AKA they don't review the data that they're looking at, they're not asking the right level of questions to understand the data that they're reviewing, etc.

#### What's something you do differently from others, and why does it work better?

I spend a lot of time with people that report directly to me and indirectly to me. This means a higher level of cadence with the people that report directly to me, and then a lower level of cadence of one-on-ones with people that indirectly report to me. That has worked really well for me because I get to understand the people at a personal level and some of the challenges they may be faced with professionally and personally, and how I can help them navigate those challenges better.

#### What's an idea or method you often share that makes people say, "I never saw it that way before"?

I often talk about using a **leveraged model** when you're trying to determine the type of talent you have available in your organization. The leverage model consists of really three layers:

- **Layer 1:** The most senior resources that are available to you that can do all the work you want them to.
- **Layer 2:** People that can do sophisticated work but who aspire to be at Level 1.
- **Layer 3:** People who can do a lot of the operational tasks and aspire to be in Level 2.

Using the leverage model, you can determine who can move from which level to which level, and ultimately it will give you an equation where you can determine how many people you have to train, how many people you have to top-grade, and how many people you would actually have to hire from the outside. That model has worked for me for the last 25 years.

#### What's one thing you wish your clients or peers understood sooner?

I think making transformational changes requires the ability to look into the future and dissipate some of the risks, and then have game plans for how to react to those risks and adjust on an incremental basis. If clients get good at that, they can really achieve the best results for their organization.

#### When did things finally start working better for you — what changed?

I think for me, the things that started working better were when I stopped paying attention to what other people think. That really changed my perspective of what I wanted to achieve in my personal and professional life.

#### What "rule" in your industry do you think needs to be broken?

I think there are a couple of rules, but I would say taking risks is really important, and not always putting up with status quo.

#### What's one belief you used to hold that you no longer agree with, and what changed your mind?

It's important to give second chances. I've had situations where I gave no chances to people only to find out that I misjudged the person and should have given them a second chance. And when it comes to customers, I say the same thing — pursuing customers multiple times to make sure that they actually come to you.

#### What habit or mindset helps you stay consistent and grounded in your work?

I think one of the mindsets is believing in the work that I do each day.

#### What's one story that best shows the results or impact of your work?

I would say one of the relevant stories that shows the results of my work and its impact would be taking a one-time revenue services organization and converting it into a subscription-based organization, which contributed to **60% of the revenue coming from subscription and 40% of the revenue coming from one-time**. This created a higher valuation for the services organization, but it also influenced the overall ARR for the company — that at Imperva.

#### How has your idea of success changed over time?

The idea of success has certainly changed over time. I think in the past it was all about how quickly I can move up in my career, how quickly I can make more money — to now, where it's being around the people that I want to be around with and having real conversations. I think money is still important, but it's no longer something that I look at as the definition of success.

#### Where do you think your industry or space is headed in the next few years?

Well, I think the industry from a cybersecurity perspective will just continue to evolve, get complicated, and prosper. But if I think about it more from a customer success perspective, I do believe that the success organization will be the currency of expansion of customers — it will be the most predictable organization to forecast ARR and NDR.

### Phase 3: Authority & Conversion

**Goal:** Show proof, process, and transformation in simple, human ways.

#### What system or routine have you built that makes your work or your clients' lives easier?

Well, I use a framework which requires about **8 weeks** of an engagement with workshops, interviews, and readouts to discuss the current state of the organization — what are the potential gaps, and how to achieve those gaps with an implementation plan. With that implementation plan, I want to be able to work with the customer and get them there more from a strategic perspective and a little bit light on the operational stuff.

#### When people first reach out to you, what do they usually worry about, and how do you help them feel safe or clear?

I don't have too many examples, but I would say a company that is in a startup phase trying to figure out what a customer success organization should do, and what is a phased approach where they need to get to.

#### What's one simple tip or idea that helps people get small wins fast?

Well, if an organization is looking to transform, the best advice would be to build a plan that shows value fairly quickly on the front end and then incrementally over a certain time. So let's say run an 8-week engagement which provides clear outcomes and plans of what needs to happen over, let's say, the next 12 months, and key milestones.

#### What change do people usually see after working with you, and what helped them reach that point?

There are several changes, but I would say that customers get clarity around their customer success strategy, and then they also get a framework that tells them the steps they need to make to achieve the results.

#### Can you share a story that shows how your work made a real difference for someone?

Even though I don't have a specific customer, I think the best way to describe it is for companies that I have worked at — Aperture, Emerson Network Power, SDG, and Imperva — they all had challenges in how to grow top-line new logos and then holding on to their customers. I was able to work with each one of these companies to first do an assessment and maturity of the organization, then identify the gaps and low-hanging fruit, and then implement a plan in a phased approach with targeted milestones and outcomes to achieve for each organization.

#### What's a question people often ask before they say yes to working with you, and how do you answer it?

The most often question people ask is: "Can you show me how to set up a customer success function from the ground up?" My answer is yes, but we have to take it in a phased approach.
